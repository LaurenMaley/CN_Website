1.1-Describing the purpose of IT in modern business.

From personal experience I know how imperative IT is in the running of an international company, more specifically a retail business. Every aspect of running a company now relies on seamless IT being implemented throughout the business. Stock systems in the modern day rely heavily on the IT systems in place to track stock. From stock held in main warehouses being distributed to each of the stores as well as each store's personal warehouses. Point of sale (POS) systems connected to the stock systems allow companies to track trends in their sales and review popularity of products, utilizing this information to aid the business. An online presence whether it be via a website or social networking sites, is crucial for advertising, expanding your customer base and potentially online sales.[1] Without this it is increasingly difficult for a company to thrive in the modern IT based climate, this has been proven as many companies have gone under during the global covid pandemic as they were unable to implement online services or sales. Furthermore, for smaller businesses social networking sites can act as free advertising if paying for online advertising or a website is out of budget. Additionally, new technology gives smaller businesses and online businesses easier access to online payments through fund transfer services like Paypal[2] and the ability to accept card payments through mobile apps and devices like iZettle[3].

1.4-Describe the risks that might impact digital projects

Digital projects like any projects can suffer negatively from human error. However, an additional element of a digital project means that as well as human error you also have to account for the technology you’re using failing too. An example of this would be if the internet connection is weak or fails, your project would be delayed, especially if you haven’t previously set up an offline mode. The risk of digital projects is working with technology that can be unpredictable, computers can crash randomly and is unpredictable, this can lead to the loss of work. The only way to counter this would be to save work frequently and periodically. Lack of knowledge and planning can also hinder a digital project, therefore it’s important to research and plan before starting your project. Whilst you can research during a project it takes away from time that could be used working on the project itself.

Selecting the correct technology for you to utilize during your project is wholly dependent on you and your project's requirements. If you are creating a project by yourself you are more likely to do a lot of travelling and therefore a laptop would be a more astute choice as it is portable and would mean you are able to work on the go, thereby increasing your efficiency. Additionally, as with any digital project it is likely a stable internet would be required in order for the project to be completed smoothly, whether that be for the project itself or to aid in communication.

1.7-Describe legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects

From May 2018, the EU enacted legislation known as GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) which has become known as the strictest privacy and information law in the world. Essentially it regulates all personal data collected and stored by companies for the protection of individuals against data crimes that may be committed if the data is mishandled. For companies and digital projects this can mean implementing “technical measures meaning anything from requiring your employees to use two-factor authentication on accounts where personal data are stored to contracting with cloud providers that use end-to-end encryption.” [12]. As for employees and colleagues working on digital projects it is vital hey have an understanding of GDPR and the individual companies data privacy policy. Additionally, limiting who has access to this information within a team can also help. Companies and employees are to be informed and held accountable for their handling of personal data and failure to do so can result in penalties and fines, “which max out at €20 million or 4% of global revenue (whichever is higher), plus data subjects have the right to seek compensation for damages” [12].
