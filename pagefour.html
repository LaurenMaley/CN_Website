1.5 Describing copyright and other constraints affecting websites

Copyright is a legal protection for who owns created content for all types of creative work and whilst this does not include industrial or technological inventions it can include computer software as a creative work, which means that it can be considered protected under copyright. Sometimes copyright and intellectual property overlap but “websites contain written material, graphics, business logos, and other visual and sound elements. This firmly categorizes them as copyrightable material” [19]. Whilst each country's law may differ slightly, in the UK according to the Copyright (Computer Programs) Regulations 1992, websites are protected as soon as they are published, including trademarks. Therefore, copyright will affect the images, written material and any video or sound among other content that you have not yourself created but intend to publish on your website.


1.6 Describing access issues that need taking into account

Whilst advances in technology have helped technology itself become more accessible to all. The vast majority of websites are still not accessible to people with certain conditions. Epilepsy sufferers for example may find websites with flashing video or animation sets off their condition, therefore ideally a flash warning should be displayed for these users. For people with vision related disabilities and complete blindness, accessing the internet could be potentially impossible. However, thankfully there are technologies such as screen readers [20], that read websites aloud to users that are vision impared or blind. Screen readers are aided by the use of alt tags where images are concerned and therefore for a screen reader technology to be as effective as possible alt tags should be as descriptive as possible in order to be accessible for vision impaired and blind website users. For elderly users and those with learning difficulties it is best to keep your text simple and break up large chunks of text so they are more readable. Whilst the aforementioned users may benefit from the readability of black text on a white background users with dyslexia will find that harder to read so a yellow tinged white background would be a more astute choice. There are lots of solutions to making online websites more accessible for people with disabilities, it's simply a case of knowing who your audience is, doing research as to how you can better accommodate them and making adjustments based on that information [21].


1.7 Describe which are the appropriate filetypes for websites

All file types have their own advantages and disadvantages to being saved as such. An example of this image file types, which can be broken down into three comparison points: performance, appearance and scalability. These are the rate at which images load on your site, how high of a quality the image is displayed in and how the image looks on different sized screens. The most common image file types are JPEG, PNG and GIF. Whilst JPEG and PNG are non animated images,with JPEG being preferable for images with a higher colour saturation, and PNG being better utilized for images where scalability will come into use. GIF’s are animated images so whilst they hold a unique advantage over the other two file types in that sense, their downfall is that image quality and scalability can sometimes suffer as a result of using the GIF file type [22]. In terms of website creation, having used CSS and HTML to create my website they are saved as their own file types respectively. Another common file type that you will come across when using websites is an EXE file type used to install a downloaded program. You should be weary of this file type though as unfortunately sometimes they may contain viruses [23].
